    13.15.4.10 Dealing with debt emergencies

    Your client has a debt emergency if theyâ€™re facing immediate action because of a debt. For example, they might:

    • have bailiffs at or coming to their home to take goods
    • be about to be evicted from their home or have their home repossessed
    • be about to have their gas or electricity disconnected
    • be about to have hire purchase goods repossessed
    • have received a summons and be due in court in the very near future
    • have been issued with an arrest warrant and threatened with imprisonment
    • have a creditor who is trying to make them bankrupt

    Your client is most likely to have a debt emergency if they have unpaid priority debts.

    A non-priority debt could also lead to a debt emergency if your client doesnâ€™t stick to a repayment plan or respond to letters from a creditor.

    In a debt emergency, the priority is to stop or delay the imminent action. If making a payment will delay the action, make sure itâ€™s a payment that your client can afford.

    Check deadlines

    If your client might be in a debt emergency, check if their creditors are about to take action.

    If action is imminent, itâ€™s a debt emergency. What you need to do next depends on the type of emergency.

    If thereâ€™s no imminent action, your client should collect information about all of their debts so they can start dealing with their debts.

    Eviction or repossession

    If bailiffs are coming to evict your client or repossess their property, check:

    • what paperwork your client has received
    • the date and time of the eviction
    • your client's housing status if theyâ€™re renting, to confirm their rights and their landlordâ€™s rights
    • if your client is selling the property they have the mortgage arrears on

    If bailiffs are at the door

    Your client will need to pack their belongings and leave. If they donâ€™t, thereâ€™s a risk they could be arrested. Make sure your client knows what bailiffs coming to evict tenants can and can't do.

    If your client has nowhere to stay, check if they can get homelessness help.

    If bailiffs are coming soon

    You can use form N244 to apply to get the warrant of possession suspended, postponed or set aside before bailiffs arrive if:

    • your client can offer to pay the arrears - the court wonâ€™t suspend the warrant if your client canâ€™t pay
    • your client is selling the property and has a buyer - the court might agree to suspend the warrant so the sale can go through

    You can apply using form N244 until the day before the planned eviction.

    You should immediately also:

    • ask the landlord or mortgage lender not to oppose your clientâ€™s application to suspend the warrant
    • check if your client can get homelessness help if theyâ€™re evicted and have nowhere to stay

    If your client is at risk of eviction or repossession and youâ€™re unsure what to do next, you should get specialist housing advice.

    If bailiffs are coming to recover a debt, check:

    • who the bailiffs are
    • what theyâ€™re visiting for - for example council tax arrears or to enforce a high court judgment
    • if your client is liable for the debt
    • the date on the notice of enforcement if your client has one - bailiffs canâ€™t come within 7 days of this date
    • the notice of enforcement is valid and not from a debt collector
    • if your client is vulnerable - there are rules about how bailiffs should treat vulnerable clients

    If bailiffs are at the door

    Your client should not let them in. Check how to stop bailiffs at the door.

    Contact the creditor to tell them your client is getting advice. Find the creditor contact details if you donâ€™t have them.

    Ask for time to prepare a full financial statement so your client can make an affordable payment offer.

    If your client has let bailiffs in

    Tell your client what bailiffs can take and how to stop bailiffs taking things they shouldnâ€™t

    If your client can afford to pay the debt off in full, they should arrange this with the bailiffs to stop them taking goods. 

    If your client canâ€™t afford to pay the debt in full, they might be able to make a controlled goods agreement to stop the bailiffs taking goods.

    If bailiffs are coming for judgment debts

    Your client might be able to apply to get the warrant of control suspended if bailiffs are coming because of a county court judgment or a high court judgment. 

    The process for doing this is the same as for changing the terms of payment on a court judgment. 

    Your client should complete form N245 for a county court judgment and form N244 for a high court judgment.

    Your client will have to pay a fee to apply unless they are on a low income or certain benefits. Read about help with court fees on GOV.UK.

    A creditor is threatening to repossess hire purchase goods

    If your client has bought goods on a hire purchase agreement but has missed payments, the creditor might be able to repossess the goods. Check:

    • if your client received a default notice - the creditor has to send this before they can repossess the goods
    • if your client has paid more than one third of the cost of the goods - if they have, the goods are protected and canâ€™t be repossessed without a court order unless your client agrees to give them back
    • the date on the warrant of delivery, if your client has been told the creditor has a court order and bailiffs are coming for the goods

    If the goods are not protected and your client agrees to give them back, or the goods are on public property, the creditor can take them without needing a court order.

    Action you can take

    If your client has been told the creditor is coming to take back the goods you might be able to:

    • contact the creditor and ask for your client to be given more time to pay
    • contact the creditor and arrange for your client to pay off the debt in full
    • arrange a time order so your client can pay off the debt in installments - you should get specialist advice from Shelter to help your client do this

    If your client has been told the creditor has a court order and bailiffs are coming to take the goods you should:

    • apply to the court to have the warrant of delivery suspended - you should try and make an agreement with the creditor before doing this
    • make sure your client knows that bailiffs can only take the item mentioned on the warrant of delivery

    Court summons for debt

    If your client has been told they have to go to court for a debt, check:

    • what the debt is for
    • what paperwork your client has been sent, for example a summons for non-payment of council tax
    • the dates of any court hearings
    • any deadlines for your client to respond or send forms to the court or to the creditor

    Action you can take

    Depending on your clientâ€™s situation you could:

    Arrest warrants for debt

    If your client has been sent an arrest warrant, check:

    • what the debt is - it might be an unpaid fine, child maintenance or council tax
    • what paperwork your client has been sent about the debt
    • whether the warrant is with or without bail
    • the date for any committal hearings that have been arranged already
    • the status of the debt and whether your client can make any payments towards it

    Action you can take

    If your client has come for advice on the day of their hearing, they should gather as much information as they can about their financial situation. This can include bank statements, wage slips or gas and electricity bills.

    They might be asked to fill in a form at the hearing to help work out what they can afford to pay. Make sure your client knows they should only offer to pay what they can afford.

    If your client has a warrant with bail, they should follow the instructions on the warrant and visit the court to be given a time and date for a hearing.

    If your client has a warrant without bail, they should go to the court as soon as possible - theyâ€™ll be arrested and held until a hearing can be organised.

    Preparing for a committal hearing

    If your client's hearing is soon, prepare a full financial statement for them to show at the hearing. 

    If your client can show they didnâ€™t have any money to pay, the court could write off the debt.

    If your client has some money to pay the debt, the court will usually suspend the order so your client can try to pay the debt off in installments. If your client doesnâ€™t keep up the repayments they'll be brought back to court.
